### Expected Value

**Discrete random variables**

Weighted using the PMF:

$$
E[X] = \sum_{x} x \cdot P_X(x)
$$

**Continuous random variables**

Weighted using the PDF:

$$
E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \cdot f_X(x) \, dx
$$

### Variance

Variance is the expected value of the square minus the square of the expected value:

$$
\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2
$$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent:

$$
\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)
$$

### Standard Deviation

$$
\text{SD}(X) = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}
$$

### Skewness and Kurtosis

Consider two examples:

In case 1, you buy a $1 lottery ticket: 99% of the time you lose $1, and 1% of the time you win $99.

In case 2, you are an insurance company selling a $1 car insurance policy: 99% of the time you gain $1, and 1% of the time you lose $99.

In both cases, the expected return and variance are identical — so how do we tell them apart?

We can use $E[X^3]$, the third moment, to distinguish them:

![Screenshot 2026-07-21 at 11.58.06 PM.png](/images/probability-&-statistic-in-machine-learning(ii)/Screenshot_2026-07-21_at_11.58.06_PM.png)

The formal definition of skewness is:

$$
\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{E[(X - \mu)^3]}{\sigma^3}
$$

Here's another example to illustrate kurtosis:

**Case 1**

- 0.5 probability of winning $1
- 0.5 probability of losing $1

**Case 2**

- 100/202 probability of winning 10 cents
- 100/202 probability of losing 10 cents
- 1/202 probability of winning $10
- 1/202 probability of losing $10

This example illustrates why kurtosis matters: both cases can share the same mean and variance, yet the fourth moment (kurtosis) reveals the difference in tail risk.

### Marginal and Conditional Distribution

**Marginal Distribution**

Discrete case:

$$
P_X(x) = \sum_{y} P(X=x, Y=y)
$$

Continuous case:

$$
f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y) \, dy
$$

**Conditional Distribution**

Discrete case:

$$
P(X=x \mid Y=y) = \frac{P(X=x, Y=y)}{P(Y=y)}
$$

Continuous case:

$$
f(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x,y)}{f_Y(y)}
$$


### Covariance & Covariance Matrix

$$
\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]
$$

If independent:

$$
\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) = 0.0
$$

For a random vector, the covariance matrix collects the pairwise covariances between components:

$$
\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)
$$

### Correlation Coefficient

Looking at covariance alone can be misleading about the strength of a relationship, since it isn't scale-invariant. The correlation coefficient normalizes by the standard deviations:

$$
\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}
$$

### Multivariate Gaussian Distribution

Real-world data is often generated by multiple correlated variables jointly, which is where the multivariate Gaussian distribution comes in:

$$
f_X(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^k |\Sigma|}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\mu)\right)
$$
