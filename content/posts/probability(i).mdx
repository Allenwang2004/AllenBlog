### What is probability?

Ex: If there are ten child, three of them know how to play soccer. Find the probability that a child picked at randoms play soccer.

Ans:

$$
P(soccer) = \frac{soccer}{total} = \frac{3}{10}
$$

### Complement of Probability

According to the previous example. Fine the probability of child NOT playing soccer.

Ans:

$$
P(notsoccer) = 1-P(soccer) = 1-\frac{3}{10}=\frac{7}{10}
$$

### Sum of Probability

Consider the joint event. Kids can play as many sports as they want. What is the probability that childs play soccer or basketball?

Ans:

$$
P(soccer \; \cup \;basketball) = P(soccer) + P(basketball) - p(soccer \; \cap basketball)

$$

Note that these events areВ **mutually exclusive**, in other words, you cannot have a car simuntaneously in two doors, because of the rules of the game. This means that,

рқ‘ғ(рқҗё1 вҲ© рқҗё2)=0,В рқ‘ғ(рқҗё1 вҲ© рқҗё3)=0В andВ рқ‘ғ(рқҗё2 вҲ© рқҗё3) = 0.

Consider a more complex situation

Now, the game is:

- There areВ рқ‘ӣ doors, and you must choose one door.
- Host opensВ рқ‘ҳ doors and revealing goats.
- You may or may not change your previously chosen door.

The question is: is it always better to switch doors? Will it depend onВ рқ‘ҳ?

To answer this question analyticaly, first define the following events:

- рқҗёрқ‘–=В the car is behind door i. In this case,В рқ‘–=1,вҖҰ,рқ‘ӣ.

Again, theВ рқҗёрқ‘–'s are independent from each other, because there is onlyВ 1В car available.

Note that, since the Host never opens the same door the player chose and also never opens the winning door, there is an upper bound forВ рқ‘ҳ, which isВ рқ‘ӣвҲ’2, so 0вүӨрқ‘ҳвүӨрқ‘ӣвҲ’2.

Two facts can be assumed:

- The player chooses door 1
- The host opens doors 2,вҖҰ,рқ‘ҳ+1

This is because we can always rename the doors to get this result. For instance, if the player chooses door numberВ 10, we can rename it as doorВ 1В and doorВ 1В will become doorВ 10. This is just to avoid getting too complex on indices notations. In math terminology, it is usually said that we can do thisВ *without loss of generality*, since it will not affect the final result.

Now that there areВ рқ‘ӣВ doors, the probability that the car is behind doorВ 1В isВ 1/рқ‘ӣ, i.e.,

рқ‘ғ(рқҗё1)=1/рқ‘ӣ.

By the complement rule, the probability that the car isВ **not**В behind doorВ 1В is:

рқ‘ғ(рқҗёрқ‘җ1)=1вҲ’рқ‘ғ(рқҗё1)=1вҲ’1/рқ‘ӣ=рқ‘ӣвҲ’1/рқ‘ӣ.

Note that

рқҗёрқ‘җ1 = рқҗё2вҲӘрқҗё3вҲӘвҖҰвҲӘрқҗёрқ‘ӣ.

There is a notation to simplify the right hand side equation above, we can write it as:

$$
вӢғ_{рқ‘–=2}^рқ‘ӣрқҗё_рқ‘–

This works in the same fashion as a summation symbol, but the opeartion being performed is set union.

So, we know that

$$
рқ‘ғ(вӢғ_{рқ‘–=2}^рқ‘ӣрқҗё_рқ‘–)=\frac{рқ‘ӣвҲ’1}{рқ‘ӣ}.

Now we can answer the question: What is the probability of winning, given that we switch doors?

If the player switches to a random available door, then they must choose one of theВ рқ‘ҳ+2,рқ‘ҳ+3,вҖҰ,рқ‘ӣвҲ’1,рқ‘ӣ. Therefore, the probability of picking the car is:

The probability ofВ **not picking the car**В in doorВ 1В (рқ‘ғ(рқҗёрқ‘җ1)=рқ‘ӣвҲ’1/рқ‘ӣ)В times the probability of picking the carВ **now**, which isВ 1рқ‘ӣвҲ’рқ‘ҳвҲ’1В because this is the number of remaining doors.

And the equality only holds whenВ рқ‘ҳ=0. This means that the host does not open any door.

Therefore,В **it is always better to switch doors**. This may sound counterintuitive at first, but think that switching doors you are using theВ **new piece of information**В that the host gave you, whereas if you choose not to switch, you will be ignoring this new information.

### Discrete Probability Distribution

#### PMF (Probability Mass Function)

For a discrete random variable $X$, the PMF is defined as:

$$
P_X(x) = P(X = x)
$$

**Properties of PMF:**

**1. Non-negativity**

$$
P_X(x) \geq 0 \text{ for all } x
$$

**2. Normalization (Completeness)**

The sum of all probabilities must equal 1:

$$
\sum_{x} P_X(x) = 1
$$

**Proof:**

Since $X$ must take on one of its possible values, all possible outcomes form the sample space:

$$
\{X = x_1\} \cup \{X = x_2\} \cup \cdots \cup \{X = x_n\} = \Omega
$$

These events are mutually exclusive (disjoint), so by the law of total probability:

$$
P(\Omega) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(X = x_i) = 1
$$

### Continuous Probability Distribution

#### PDF (Probability Density Function)

For a continuous random variable $X$, the PDF is denoted as $f_X(x)$. Unlike PMF, the PDF does not represent the probability at a point. Instead, it describes the relative likelihood of values.

**Key Properties:**

**1. Non-negativity**

$$
f_X(x) \geq 0 \text{ for all } x
$$

**2. Normalization (Total Probability)**

The total area under the curve equals 1:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) \, dx = 1
$$

**Proof:**

The probability that $X$ takes any value in its range must be 1:

$$
P(-\infty < X < \infty) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) \, dx = 1
$$

**3. Probability over an Interval**

For any interval $[a, b]$:

$$
P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_{a}^{b} f_X(x) \, dx
$$

#### CDF (Cumulative Distribution Function)

The CDF is a universal concept defined for both discrete and continuous random variables:

$$
F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)
$$

**For Discrete Random Variables:**

$$
F_X(x) = \sum_{x_i \leq x} P_X(x_i)
$$

**For Continuous Random Variables:**

$$
F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) \, dt
$$

**Properties of CDF:**

**1. Monotonicity**

If $a < b$, then:

$$
F_X(a) \leq F_X(b)
$$

**Proof:**

If $a < b$, then the event $\{X \leq a\}$ is a subset of $\{X \leq b\}$:

$$
\{X \leq a\} \subset \{X \leq b\} \implies P(X \leq a) \leq P(X \leq b)
$$

**2. Boundary Conditions**

$$
\lim_{x \to -\infty} F_X(x) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to \infty} F_X(x) = 1
$$

**3. Relationship between CDF and PDF**

For a continuous random variable, the PDF is the derivative of the CDF:

$$
f_X(x) = \frac{dF_X(x)}{dx}
$$

**Proof:**

By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:

$$
F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) \, dt \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{dF_X(x)}{dx} = f_X(x)
$$

**4. Probability Calculation using CDF**

For any interval $(a, b]$:

$$
P(a < X \leq b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a)
$$

**Proof:**

$$
F_X(b) - F_X(a) = P(X \leq b) - P(X \leq a) = P(a < X \leq b)
$$
